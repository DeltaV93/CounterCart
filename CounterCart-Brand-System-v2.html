Part I
BRAND STRATEGY
01
Origin

Brand Story

CounterCart exists because spending and values are usually in conflict. People buy coffee from companies they disagree with. They fill their carts at stores that don't reflect who they are. They feel it — the low hum of cognitive dissonance at checkout. CounterCart doesn't ask people to stop spending. It asks them to offset it.

The name says it: counter your cart. Every purchase can trigger a micro-donation to causes that matter. Not guilt — agency. Not punishment — participation. The round-up model is simple by design. Complexity is the enemy of follow-through.

CounterCart was born from the idea that consumer behavior is the largest untapped channel for social impact. Not because people don't care, but because caring is too hard. We make it automatic.

You don't have to change how you shop. Just change what it means.
— CounterCart positioning statement

Turn passive spending into active generosity through frictionless micro-donations.
Vision

A world where every transaction carries a thread of intentional giving — where spending and values are never in conflict.

NO FRICTION. NO GUILT.
We never shame, lecture, or moralize. We make participation effortless and transparent.
Position
THE OFFSET ENGINE
Not a charity app. Not a spending tracker. The invisible layer between your wallet and your conscience.

And the Everyperson — the one who believes change should be accessible to everyone, not just the privileged few. Together they form a brand personality that's sharp but not aggressive, opinionated but not preachy, and accessible without being bland.

03
Who We Serve

Audience

Our primary audience is the conscious pragmatist — ages 25 to 45, digitally native, values-driven but busy. They donate when they remember. They feel a twinge when they see their Amazon order history. They want to do more but won't add another to-do list item. CounterCart is for the person who says "I care" but needs the path of least resistance to prove it.

Primary
THE CONSCIOUS PRAGMATIST
25–45. Urban or suburban. Makes $50K–$150K. Shops online heavily. Wants to align spending with values but has zero extra time. Responds to clarity and confidence, not guilt.
Secondary
THE QUIET GIVER
35–60. Already donates to causes annually. Likes the idea of automating it so the giving happens without thinking. Values transparency in where the money goes.
Aspirational
THE NEXT-GEN ACTIVIST
18–28. Views consumption as political. Shares causes on social. Drawn to brands with a clear point of view. Needs to see CounterCart as a tool for agency, not charity.
Key insight: Our audience doesn't think of themselves as "charitable donors." They're spenders who want their spending to mean something. Don't talk to them like philanthropists. Talk to them like people who buy coffee every day and wish it did more.
04
How We Speak

Voice & Tone

CounterCart's voice is direct, warm, and wry. We sound like a sharp friend who happens to know a lot about impact — not a nonprofit's annual report, and not a tech startup's landing page. We use short sentences. We prefer active voice. We never use jargon. We trust that our audience is smart enough to not need everything explained twice.

Tone shifts by context — onboarding is warmer and more encouraging, transactional moments are crisp and functional, and impact reports can be more narrative and reflective. But the voice stays the same: clear-eyed, confident, human.

Vocabulary
SAY THIS
Offset, redirect, plug in, round up, your causes, your change, counter your cart, give back, spend with purpose
Vocabulary
NEVER SAY
Charitable giving, philanthropy, impact investment, optimize your giving, make a difference (™), empower, leverage, synergy, disruption
Sentence Style
SHORT. PUNCHY. DIRECT.
Lead with the verb. Front-load the value. Max 15 words for headlines, max 25 for body sentences. If it needs a comma, it might need two sentences.
Point of View
SECOND PERSON
Always "you" and "your." The user is the protagonist. We're the tool, not the hero. "Your spending. Your causes. Your terms."

Voice examples by context:

Mark

Logo & Wordmark

The CounterCart logo is a wordmark — Anton uppercase, with a small yellow icon square (the "counter" mark). The icon contains a recirculation symbol (↻) representing the round-up loop: spend, offset, return. The wordmark must always be set in Anton. No alterations, no substitutions, no lowercase.

Clear space: Minimum clear space around the logo is equal to the height of the icon mark. Never crowd the logo against edges, other logos, or body text. The logo should breathe — like everything else in this system.

Do not: Set the wordmark in any font other than Anton. Place the logo on busy backgrounds. Stretch, rotate, or add effects. Use the icon without the wordmark in external-facing contexts (icon-only is acceptable in-app after brand recognition is established).

09
Imagery

Photography & Imagery

Photography is the exception, not the rule. The CounterCart visual system is typographic and flat-color first. When photography is used, it follows strict guidelines: high contrast, candid over posed, and always treated with a warm desaturation that matches the paper palette. We don't use stock-photo smiles. We don't show hands holding phones. We show the world as it is — everyday commerce, urban texture, real people in real contexts.

☕
Everyday Commerce
Coffee shops, grocery runs, street vendors. The mundane moments where spending happens.
◐
High Contrast B&W
When used, black and white is preferred. High contrast, deep blacks, bright whites. Blue Note energy.
⬡
Urban Texture
Brick walls, transit signs, crosswalks. The built environment as backdrop — never nature stock photos.
Treatment
WARM DESATURATED
Reduce saturation 40%. Warm the shadows. Lift the blacks slightly to match the cream paper. Add 2% grain overlay. The photo should feel printed, not digital.
Composition
CANDID OVER POSED
Hands in motion. Cropped tight. Off-center subjects. No one looking at the camera. No staged diversity circles. Real moments, imperfectly captured.
Avoid
STOCK ENERGY
No smiling-at-laptop. No high-fives. No tech-company-in-a-glass-office. No hands forming a circle. No aerial drone shots of nature. If it feels like a LinkedIn banner, reject it.

Icons

Iconography

CounterCart uses a minimal icon set. Icons are monoline, 2px stroke, square-cornered, and always set in ink color. No filled icons. No colorful icons. No emoji substitutions. Icons are functional — they clarify UI actions, not decorate. When in doubt, use a word instead. Typography always wins over iconography in this system.

Movement

Motion & Animation

Motion should feel physical — like paper sliding or ink pressing. The easing curve is cubic-bezier(0.23, 1, 0.32, 1): a fast start that decelerates smoothly, like setting a book down on a table. No bouncing. No elastic. No particle effects. Three approved motion patterns:

Slide (hover me)
Lift + Shadow
Fade
Duration
FAST: 150MS
Hover states, focus rings, input changes. Immediate feedback for micro-interactions.
Duration
MID: 300MS
Card lifts, button presses, nav hovers. The standard interaction speed.
Duration
SLOW: 600MS
Page transitions, hero reveals, section fades. Reserved for significant state changes.
Motion rule: If the animation draws attention to itself, it's too much. Motion should serve comprehension, not decoration. The marquee scrolls because the content is dynamic. The card lifts because the user needs affordance. Nothing moves for fun.

Accessibility

Every color pairing in the system must meet WCAG AA minimum contrast ratios. The warm neutrals were selected specifically to maintain legibility while preserving the vintage aesthetic. Focus states use the yellow accent as an outline. All interactive elements must be keyboard-navigable.

Focus states: All focusable elements receive a 3px yellow outline offset by 2px. Buttons show the shadow-ink lift on focus. Inputs shift their border to yellow. The grain overlay uses pointer-events: none so it never intercepts clicks or focus.

Motion sensitivity: Respect prefers-reduced-motion. The marquee should stop. Card lifts should be instant. Page transitions should be immediate. Only the grain overlay (static, non-moving) persists.

18
Ethos

Design Principles

Paper First, Color Earned
Paper and ink carry the design. Yellow and blue appear at decisive moments — a CTA, an accent stripe, a badge. If you removed all color, the design should still work. Color is the punctuation, not the sentence.
Two Colors. That's It.
Yellow and powder blue do all the accent work. Everything else is ink and paper. Reid Miles proved that constraint produces distinctiveness — honor that discipline.
Poster, Not App
Every screen should feel like it could be ripped off and stapled to a telephone pole. Big type, clear hierarchy, minimal elements. If you can't read it from across the room, the type isn't big enough.
Flat Is the Point
No gradients, no depth effects. Hard offset shadows only. The aesthetic is flat because ink on paper is flat. Screen-print energy — one color layered on another, nothing blended.
Type Does the Heavy Lifting
Anton at 64px is the illustration. You don't need icons, images, or decorative elements when the letterforms are this bold. Typography is the visual identity.
Warm the Neutrals
Paper is cream, not white. Ink is warm black, not pure #000. This warmth shift separates "vintage confidence" from "tech startup." It's the yellowed page, the aged poster.
Confidence, Not Aggression
The yellow is warm and inviting, not warning-sign urgent. The blue is calming, not corporate. CounterCart states, it doesn't yell. Quiet confidence, always.
19
Guardrails

Do's & Don'ts

✓ Do
Let paper and ink carry 80% of any composition
Use yellow/blue as accent stripes, bars, and badges — not full backgrounds
Keep Anton always uppercase — it's designed for it
Use hard offset shadows only (3px 3px, no blur)
Let typography be the visual — big, bold, structural
Write copy that's punchy and direct — short sentences, active voice
Use Courier Prime for all body and UI text
Apply texture sparingly — 3% opacity max for grain
Think "poster on a telephone pole" for every composition
Use cream paper backgrounds, never pure white
✗ Don't
Fill entire sections with yellow or blue backgrounds
Use gradients, glass-morphism, or blurred shadows
Scatter small color accents — yellow as tiny dots everywhere
Add illustrations, custom icons, or decorative imagery
Use Inter, Roboto, SF Pro, or any clean sans-serif
Center-align body text — left-align like a manuscript
Use emoji or playful animations
Make anything feel "tech startup" or "charity nonprofit"
Use jargon: "philanthropy," "empower," "leverage," "synergy"
Over-design — if a sign painter wouldn't do it, skip it
